Inspiration

Fraud doesn't break in. It gets invited in.

The fastest-growing scams don't crack encryption — they crack people. A caller talks someone into reading out a one-time code. A stranger glances at a phone on the train and memorizes a card number. A text with the bank's name links to a login that isn't the bank's.

Every one of those attacks succeeds in the seconds before anyone realizes it's happening. By the time you reach a fraud hotline, the money's gone. We wanted the defense to live where the attack does: on the screen, in the moment, fast enough to matter.

So we built a banking app that watches its own back — and hands the panic button to the person being targeted.

What it does

Four ways it sees the scam coming

Every account view carries a live risk score — a speedometer that climbs the moment something looks wrong and eases back down once it's handled.

BlindSpot · shoulder-surf

It hides your money when someone's watching

BlindSpot runs the front camera through an on-device face detector. The instant a second face enters the frame, the balance, card number and every transaction blur behind frosted glass and the risk needle jumps — no tap required. Look away and the details return, but the score stays elevated with a “recently exposed” flag.

Risk engine · live

A threat meter you can actually read

Green, yellow, orange, red. Each signal — an exposure, a leaked credential, a strange login — feeds a scoring model that drives one honest gauge. When the needle pins into the red, the app stops being subtle and takes over the screen.

Fraud Shield · intercept

It catches the login you didn't make

When a sign-in arrives from a device the account has never seen, Sidekick freezes it and throws a full-screen challenge to the real phone: “We blocked a sign-in from a device we don't recognize. Was this you?” One tap decides the intruder's fate.

One-tap lockdown

Victim → Defender, in one button

“LOCK IT DOWN” runs the whole containment playbook in a single gesture: session revoked, new payees cancelled, contact changes reverted, credentials rotated — each checked off in sequence. The person who was being scammed is now the one who ended it.

The attack, end to end

Watch a takeover fail in real time

Two windows, synced live — the attacker's laptop on one side, the victim's phone on the other. Here's the whole sequence the judges saw.

1

The attacker has everything

Stolen username, password and a fresh one-time code — all pre-filled on a convincing EasyWeb login. This is what credential theft actually looks like from the other side.

2

The trap springs

Credentials “accept” with green checks — then the session stalls forever on Verifying device…. The intruder thinks they're through. They're stuck in a decoy.

3

The phone lights up

The victim's device instantly registers the foreign sign-in: risk slams to 90, and the Fraud Shield challenge slides up over everything.

4

The kill switch

One tap on LOCK IT DOWN runs the containment checklist and drops the score back to safe. The account is sealed in under a second.

5

The attacker is evicted

The same event that saved the victim reaches back across to the laptop and kills it mid-session: Session terminated. Defense and counter-strike in one motion.

Under the hood

Built to react in milliseconds, not round-trips

The whole thing is engineered to feel instant, which meant putting the intelligence on the client and treating the two windows as one synchronized system.

Real-time bus

A live event fabric

A typed message bus over the BroadcastChannel API keeps every surface in lockstep — exposures, risk updates, auth attempts, lockdowns — with a localStorage storage-event fallback so it degrades gracefully.

On-device ML

Face detection in the browser

BlindSpot streams the webcam through a TinyFaceDetector convolutional net running entirely client-side at 2 Hz. Face counts drive the exposure state machine — no frame ever leaves the device.

Risk engine

A scoring state machine

A 0–100 model ingests weighted signals, clamps and decays over time, and maps to four severity bands. One source of truth drives the gauge, the blur layer, and every escalation.

Motion

60fps, hand-tuned

The speedometer needle, the sliding sheets and the lockdown checklist are pure CSS transforms on a spring easing — GPU-composited, reduced-motion aware, zero animation libraries.

Architecture

Zero-backend by design

A React 18 + Vite single-page app with hash routing and no framework bloat. All state is client-side, which is exactly why the threat-to-lockdown path clocks in under a second.

Delivery

Ships itself

Every push to main triggers a GitHub Actions pipeline that builds the static bundle and publishes to GitHub Pages — the live demo is always the latest commit.

// Engineering notes — challenges we actually hit

Two windows, one truth. Getting an exposure on the phone and a termination on the laptop to feel like a single event meant designing an idempotent message protocol and a shared reducer, so no window could drift out of state.

ML that fails invisibly. Cameras get denied, models fail to load, lighting is bad. The detection loop is fully sandboxed — if it can't run, the app never notices and every other defense keeps working.
